Building An Opportunity Machine

Anyone can "work".

As long as we're alive, we can expend effort. And as long as we can expend effort, we can find a way to make it feel productive.

But some kinds of work are rarer.

They're given leverage (for better or worse) by the reputations of people involved in the project, which can massively increase upside.

We call these kinds of work opportunities. And historically, opportunities have been extremely unevenly distributed.

But what if we could bend the rules?

How might we create our own opportunities from nothing? More importantly, how might we build a compounding vehicle, a machine, that cranks out opportunities for us?

Consider the following algorithm:

1. Accept opportunity
2. Overdeliver
3. Compound reputation to expand options
4. Choose next opportunity

If these four things occur, we effectively become unstoppable.

This gives us four problems to solve: (1) how to get the first opportunity, (2) how to overdeliver, (3) how to compound reputation, and (4) which opportunities to take.

The first of these problems is difficult (and sometimes unfair). As a general rule, it seems like most opportunities go to people who could succeed without them - iteration speed and pain tolerance are both useful means of proving this.

Overdelivering is domain-specific. Because it's so general, we can only really describe it in terms of fundamental skills: thinking from principles of nature and human nature, and operating in high-uncertainty domains. Since opportunities grow exponentially, we tend to gravitate towards things we're good at.

Compounding vehicles for reputation seem more subtle. At a basic level, our innate drive for reciprocity achieves this to some extent, but extra feedback loops can be added with media as needed.

Finally: choosing the right opportunity. This is the ultimate question. There's no single correct answer, but the more choices we make, the better we get at choosing. Crucially: any choice is better than none (and usually better than multiple conflicting ones).

That's everything. All the components of our opportunity machine.

Next: let's think more about which opportunities we should be looking for in the first place.
