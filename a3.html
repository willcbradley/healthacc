Where Are The Frontiers?

Clinical medicine seems to be a zero-sum game.

Doctors specialise as a means of dividing up existing treatments — the immediate incentive is to accrue political bargaining power (and thus expand one's "scope of practice") rather than growing the total scope of practice of humanity.

This is pretty bleak.

If our future consists of nothing more than an elaborate popularity contest, then why bother?

Thankfully, I don't think all hope is lost.

Recall our discussion of non-pointless work: we can become strategists or tacticians, informated by nature or human nature. Practitioners, scientists, entrepreneurs, or investors. Let's examine each of these frontiers in turn.

First: practice. This refers to applying existing ideas to develop new clinical insights.

While this is certainly possible, the (arguably justifiable) slowness of clinical trials and widespread adoption limit the speed of growth considerably. Despite its importance, this frontier expands too slowly to circumvent the status game.

What about science?

History is full of scientists who had outsized impacts. Why not here?

Again, this seems like an extremely important frontier of knowledge, but one that tends to move quite slowly. Are there any faster feedback loops to pursue in parallel?

Next up is business. The process of testing ideas against the market.

Right away, this seems like a faster frontier. But then again, the regulatory environment seems to bottleneck development of novel projects.

Finally, we come to investing. Again, although this is higher-leverage than building a single company, it remains constrained by regulation.

What have we learned?

Without open frontiers, things become zero-sum. Regulations can be (and are) used to uphold necessary ethical standards, but at the same time, they slow the pace of progress.

This means things inevitably tend away from immediate feedback mechanisms, and toward status games.

What to do, in the face of this?
