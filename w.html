What Is Medicine?

Things exist, somehow.

Matter combines to form increasingly complex structures with increasingly complex emergent properties. Atoms, to molecules, to cells, to tissues, and eventually, to us.

Like all living things, we strive for homeostasis. Our bodies work to maintain optimal internal environments, perfect for ongoing life.

But inevitably, entropy is on our tail.

We're constantly bombarded with disorder and decay, down to the microscopic level. Cells die, genes mutate, and we're pushed away from homeostatic stability.

We call this process stress. And when it gets out of balance, stress can cause dysfunction, disease, and suffering.

From this, we might conclude that medicine is the science of helping the body fight against entropy.

But why?

Why correct dysfunction, or cure disease, or alleviate suffering? After all, there's no physical law that forces us to.

The answer lies in prioritisation.

To perceive our surroundings, we map value-laden stories onto the world. These stories aren't rooted in physical laws, but in moral claims.

And as part of these claims, humanity has (historically) decided that reducing suffering is fundamentally good.

From this, we might conclude that medicine is the art of reducing human suffering.

But this differs from our first definition. Which is the correct answer?

Is medicine a science, or an art? Is it about fighting entropy, or relieving suffering?

Both, and neither.

At its core, medicine asks us to give scientific solutions to moral problems. We're tasked with doing what's right, while simultaneously trying to figure out what "right" means.

As the world speeds up, this tension will grow harder to manage. To retain any hope of survival, we need to attack it head-on.

We kneel before morality, then nature, in that order. All else is a suggestion.
