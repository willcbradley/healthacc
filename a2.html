Non-Pointless Work

Some work is pointless.

It solves no problems, or it solves fake problems, or some mix of both.

But if we peel away the distractions, there are surprisingly few options remaining, with surprisingly simple feedback loops.

Consider two sources of truth: nature (i.e. the physical world) and human nature (i.e. any free market). Now consider two kinds of project: strategy (which figures out what to do) and practice (which does it).

This gives us four kinds of work. Let's see how they apply to medicine.

At its core, medical school trains us to become practitioners - people who do stuff according to the natural error correction mechanism of biology. In markets, the medical equivalent would be somebody who tries to produce something of monetary value for the world (like a clinic, or a drug, or a device).

What about strategy?

In terms of nature, this would be the scientist, who discovers new ideas for practitioners to work with. In markets, these are the investors, who use capital to incentivise new developments.

Medically speaking, this gives us four paths: doctor, scientist, entrepreneur, investor. These are not mutually exclusive, but they have distinct feedback mechanisms.

This is only a broad-strokes outline (of course, there is nuance, and I'm not suggesting for a second that there are only four valid jobs).

But at least we have something to aim at.
