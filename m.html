What Is Medicine?

Nature is layered.

Matter combines to form increasingly complex structures with increasingly complex emergent properties. Atoms, to molecules, to cells, to tissues, and eventually, to us.

Like all living things, we tend toward homeostasis. Our bodies work to maintain optimal internal environments, perfect for ongoing life.

But entropy is on our tail.

We're constantly bombarded with disorder and decay, down to the microscopic level. Cells die, genes mutate, and our homeostatic stabilty is disrupted.

This disruption is what we call stress. And under the right circumstances, it can cause dysfunction, disease, and suffering.

From this, we might conclude that medicine is the science of helping the body cope with stressors.

But why?

Why correct dysfunction, or cure disease, or alleviate suffering? After all, there's no physical law forcing us to.

The answer lies in perception.

To understand our surroundings, we map value-laden stories onto the world. These stories are rooted in moral claims, rather than physical laws.

And as part of these claims, humanity has (typically) decided that reducing suffering is fundamentally good.

From this, we might conclude that medicine is the art of reducing human suffering.

But we've come up with two definitions now. Which is correct?

Is medicine a science, or an art? Is it about fighting entropy, or relieving suffering?

Both.

At its core, medicine sits between the moral and the scientific. We're tasked with doing what's right, while simultaneously trying to figure out what "right" means.

As the world speeds up, this tension will grow harder to manage. To retain any hope of survival, we need to attack it head-on.

And to do that, we need to get our priorities straight.

We're bound by morality, then nature, in that order. All else is a suggestion.
