What's The Price Of Becoming A Doctor?

Hard work is hard.

Many projects come with on-call, or night shift, or constant stress. Although these costs are real, they'll change with time and circumstances.

But now that we've defined medicine, and defined the inputs required to become a doctor, we should be able to map out the inherent (i.e. unchanging) downsides that come with the territory. Let's see what we can come up with.

Healthcare requires trust.

Insofar as doctors aren't obsolete, it's because patients rely on them during vulnerable times.

But trust scales poorly.

At a certain point, social ties become weak enough that shame can't be used to enforce the rules. So, in place of trust, we create laws and regulations.

In one sense, this is crucial. Laws provide structure, and help us move forward. But on the other hand, these rules are disconnected from reality — they're backed by power, rather than nature or markets.

The further a game is disconnected from reality, the more arbitrary the rules can become. Inevitably, things devolve into a status contest — impractical at best, corrupt and demoralising at worst.

And so, the inevitable price of becoming a doctor is playing this game. Enduring the lack of meritocracy. Putting up with arbitrary requirements. All while incurring massive opportunity cost in the process.

Luckily, this price is paid voluntarily.

If the transaction no longer makes sense, we can opt out. If we've built the right foundations, our fundamental skills can be transferred anywhere else.

Time's ticking. The world is moving too quickly for us to be blindly chasing status.

If there is any path forward, it'll be found by those who are playing the game with eyes wide open.
